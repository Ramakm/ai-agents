Space Facts: Our Universe and Solar System

The Sun
The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star located at the centre of our solar system. It accounts for 99.86% of the total mass of the entire solar system. The surface temperature of the Sun is approximately 5,500 degrees Celsius, while the core reaches around 15 million degrees Celsius. Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach Earth. The Sun generates energy through nuclear fusion, converting hydrogen into helium.

The Planets
Mercury is the smallest planet and closest to the Sun. It has extreme temperature swings from -180°C at night to 430°C during the day. Venus is the hottest planet in our solar system with surface temperatures around 465°C due to its thick carbon dioxide atmosphere. Earth is the only known planet to harbour life, with liquid water covering about 71% of its surface. Mars, the red planet, has the largest volcano in the solar system called Olympus Mons, which is nearly three times the height of Mount Everest.

Jupiter is the largest planet, with a mass greater than all other planets combined. Its Great Red Spot is a storm that has been raging for over 300 years. Saturn is famous for its stunning ring system made mostly of ice and rock. Uranus rotates on its side with an axial tilt of 98 degrees, causing extreme seasonal changes. Neptune has the fastest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of 2,100 km/h.

Black Holes
A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, including light, can escape once it crosses the event horizon. Black holes form when massive stars collapse at the end of their life cycle. The supermassive black hole at the centre of our Milky Way galaxy is called Sagittarius A*, with a mass about 4 million times that of the Sun. The first image of a black hole was captured in 2019, showing the black hole at the centre of galaxy M87.

Space Exploration
The Space Age began on October 4, 1957 when the Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1, the first artificial satellite. On July 20, 1969, Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin became the first humans to walk on the Moon during the Apollo 11 mission. The International Space Station has been continuously inhabited since November 2000. The Voyager 1 spacecraft, launched in 1977, became the first human-made object to enter interstellar space in 2012. The James Webb Space Telescope, launched in 2021, can observe galaxies formed just a few hundred million years after the Big Bang.

Stars and Galaxies
Stars are born inside massive clouds of gas and dust called nebulae. After billions of years, stars like our Sun will expand into red giants before shedding their outer layers to form planetary nebulae, leaving behind a white dwarf. Massive stars end their lives in spectacular supernova explosions. The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy containing between 200 and 400 billion stars. The nearest galaxy to our Milky Way is the Andromeda Galaxy, located about 2.5 million light-years away. The observable universe contains an estimated two trillion galaxies.

The Big Bang
The universe began approximately 13.8 billion years ago with an event known as the Big Bang. In the first fraction of a second, the universe underwent rapid inflation, expanding from a point smaller than an atom to macroscopic size. As the universe cooled, protons and neutrons formed, followed by the first atoms of hydrogen and helium. Cosmic Microwave Background radiation is the thermal afterglow of the Big Bang and can still be detected today.
