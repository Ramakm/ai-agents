World History: Key Events and Civilisations

Ancient Civilisations
The earliest known civilisations arose independently in several regions around 3000 BCE. Ancient Mesopotamia, located in modern-day Iraq between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, produced the world's first writing system called cuneiform and the Code of Hammurabi, one of the earliest law codes. Ancient Egypt flourished along the Nile River for over 3,000 years. The Egyptians built the pyramids as tombs for their pharaohs, developed hieroglyphic writing, and made advances in mathematics, medicine, and architecture. The Indus Valley Civilisation in modern-day Pakistan and India had advanced city planning, sewage systems, and standardised weights. Ancient China's Yellow River valley gave rise to the Shang and Zhou dynasties, which developed bronze casting, writing, and Confucian philosophy.

Ancient Greece and Rome
Ancient Greece, flourishing from around 800 to 146 BCE, laid foundations for Western civilisation. Greek city-states like Athens and Sparta developed democracy, philosophy, theatre, and the Olympic Games. Philosophers Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle shaped Western thought. Alexander the Great conquered a vast empire stretching from Greece to India, spreading Greek culture across the ancient world. The Roman Republic and later the Roman Empire dominated Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East for centuries. Romans built an extensive road network, developed Roman law, spread Latin language, and constructed architectural marvels including the Colosseum and the Pantheon. The fall of the Western Roman Empire in 476 CE marks the end of the ancient period.

The Middle Ages
The Middle Ages in Europe lasted from roughly 500 to 1500 CE. The feudal system organised society into lords, knights, and serfs tied to the land. The Catholic Church held enormous political and cultural power. Islamic civilisation during this period made major advances in mathematics, astronomy, medicine, and preserved ancient Greek texts. The Crusades were a series of religious wars launched by European Christians to recapture the Holy Land from Muslim rule between 1096 and 1291. The Black Death pandemic of 1347-1351 killed an estimated one-third of Europe's population. The Mongol Empire, founded by Genghis Khan in 1206, became the largest contiguous land empire in history.

The Renaissance and Age of Exploration
The Renaissance, meaning rebirth, began in Italy around the 14th century and spread across Europe. It celebrated humanism, art, and scientific inquiry. Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, and Raphael created masterpieces. The printing press invented by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440 revolutionised the spread of information. The Age of Exploration from the 15th to 17th centuries saw European powers exploring and colonising the Americas, Africa, Asia, and Oceania. Christopher Columbus reached the Americas in 1492. Vasco da Gama sailed around Africa to reach India in 1498. These voyages led to the Columbian Exchange, transferring crops, animals, and diseases between continents.

The Industrial Revolution
The Industrial Revolution began in Britain in the mid-18th century and transformed agricultural societies into industrial ones. Steam power, mechanised textile production, and iron and steel manufacturing drove rapid economic growth. The railway network expanded dramatically, connecting cities and enabling mass transportation of goods and people. Urbanisation accelerated as workers moved from rural areas to factory towns. Working conditions were often harsh, leading to the rise of labour movements and trade unions. The Industrial Revolution spread to Europe, North America, and eventually worldwide, fundamentally changing how goods were produced and how people lived.

World War One and Two
World War One from 1914 to 1918 was triggered by the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary. It became a devastating conflict involving trench warfare, poison gas, and mass casualties with approximately 20 million deaths. The Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh penalties on Germany, setting conditions for future conflict. World War Two from 1939 to 1945 began with Germany's invasion of Poland under Adolf Hitler's Nazi regime. The Holocaust systematically murdered six million Jewish people and millions of others. The United States entered after Japan's attack on Pearl Harbor in 1941. The war ended with atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki. Approximately 70-85 million people died, making it the deadliest conflict in human history.

The Cold War and Modern Era
After World War Two, the United States and Soviet Union emerged as superpowers engaged in the Cold War, a geopolitical competition that lasted until 1991. The Space Race culminated in the Moon landing in 1969. Decolonisation movements swept Asia and Africa as European empires dissolved. The United Nations was founded in 1945 to promote international cooperation and prevent future conflicts. The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 and the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991 ended the Cold War. The 21st century has been shaped by globalisation, the internet revolution, climate change challenges, and the rise of new powers like China and India.
