World geography encompasses the study of Earth's physical features, human populations, and the relationships between people and their environments. Earth's surface is divided into seven continents: Asia, Africa, North America, South America, Antarctica, Europe, and Australia. These landmasses are surrounded by five major oceans: the Pacific Ocean, the Atlantic Ocean, the Indian Ocean, the Southern Ocean, and the Arctic Ocean.

Asia is the largest continent by both area and population. It covers approximately 44.6 million square kilometers and is home to more than 4.7 billion people. The continent contains the world's highest mountain, Mount Everest, which stands at 8,849 meters above sea level in the Himalayan range on the border of Nepal and Tibet. Asia also contains the Dead Sea, the lowest land point on Earth at 430 meters below sea level. Major rivers include the Yangtze, the Yellow River, the Ganges, the Mekong, and the Ob-Irtysh system.

Africa is the second largest continent and contains 54 recognized countries. The Sahara Desert, spanning over 9 million square kilometers, is the largest hot desert in the world and stretches across the northern part of the continent. The Nile River, approximately 6,650 kilometers long, is traditionally considered the longest river in the world and flows northward through northeastern Africa into the Mediterranean Sea. The Congo River basin in central Africa contains the second largest tropical rainforest on Earth after the Amazon.

North America extends from the Arctic in the north to the tropics in the south. The Rocky Mountains run along the western portion of the continent from Canada through the United States. The Mississippi-Missouri river system drains a vast interior watershed and flows into the Gulf of Mexico. The Great Lakes, shared between the United States and Canada, form the largest group of freshwater lakes in the world by total area and hold about 21 percent of the world's surface freshwater.

South America is home to the Amazon River, which carries more water than any other river in the world and drains approximately 40 percent of the continent. The Amazon rainforest covers around 5.5 million square kilometers and is the most biodiverse tropical rainforest on Earth. The Andes Mountains, running along the western coast, form the longest continental mountain range in the world at about 7,000 kilometers. The Atacama Desert in Chile and Peru is one of the driest places on Earth, with some areas receiving less than one millimeter of rainfall per year.

Europe is the smallest continent after Australia and is characterized by a highly irregular coastline with numerous peninsulas, bays, and islands. The Alps mountain range separates southern Europe from the north. Major rivers include the Rhine, Danube, Volga, and Thames. The Volga River in Russia is the longest river in Europe at approximately 3,530 kilometers and drains into the Caspian Sea. The Mediterranean Sea, bordered by southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia, has been a crossroads of trade and culture for thousands of years.

Australia is both a country and a continent. It is the smallest continent and the largest country in the southern hemisphere. The interior of Australia is dominated by vast arid and semi-arid regions called the Outback. The Great Barrier Reef, off the northeastern coast, is the world's largest coral reef system, stretching over 2,300 kilometers. Australia contains unique wildlife including marsupials such as kangaroos, wallabies, wombats, and koalas, as well as monotremes including the platypus and echidna.

Antarctica is the southernmost continent and is covered by an ice sheet containing approximately 70 percent of Earth's fresh water. It is the coldest, driest, and windiest continent. The lowest temperature ever recorded on Earth, minus 89.2 degrees Celsius, was measured at the Soviet Vostok Station in 1983. Antarctica has no permanent human population, though research stations operated by various countries host rotating teams of scientists throughout the year.

Climate zones organize Earth's surface into regions with similar temperature and precipitation patterns. Tropical climates near the equator are warm year-round and receive high rainfall. Subtropical desert climates occupy bands around 30 degrees north and south latitude. Temperate climates have distinct seasonal variation. Continental climates experience extreme temperature differences between summer and winter. Polar climates, found at high latitudes, have very cold temperatures throughout the year and support limited vegetation.

Population is unevenly distributed across Earth's surface. The most densely populated regions include eastern China, the Indian subcontinent, western Europe, and the northeastern United States. Sparse populations are found in deserts, mountain ranges, boreal forests, and polar regions. As of 2024, Earth's human population reached approximately 8.1 billion people. The majority of the global population now lives in urban areas, a trend that began with industrialization and has continued as rural populations migrate to cities in search of economic opportunities.
