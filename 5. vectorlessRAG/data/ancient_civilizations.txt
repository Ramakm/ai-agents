Ancient civilizations formed the foundation of human history. The earliest known civilization emerged in Mesopotamia, located between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers in modern-day Iraq. The Sumerians established city-states such as Ur, Uruk, and Nippur around 3500 BCE. They developed cuneiform writing, one of the earliest writing systems, by pressing wedge-shaped marks into clay tablets. The Sumerians invented the wheel, built irrigation canals, and created one of the first known legal codes called the Code of Ur-Nammu.

Ancient Egypt rose along the Nile River in northeastern Africa. Egyptian civilization is divided into three main periods: the Old Kingdom, the Middle Kingdom, and the New Kingdom. During the Old Kingdom, pharaohs commissioned the construction of massive pyramids. The Great Pyramid of Giza, built for Pharaoh Khufu around 2560 BCE, stands 146 meters tall and remained the tallest structure in the world for over 3,800 years. Egyptians developed hieroglyphic writing, created papyrus scrolls, and made advances in medicine, astronomy, and mathematics.

The ancient Greeks made contributions across philosophy, science, mathematics, and governance. Athens became the center of intellectual life, producing thinkers such as Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle. The Greeks invented democracy as a system of government and organized the Olympic Games, first held in 776 BCE. Euclid laid the foundations of geometry, and Archimedes made key discoveries in physics and engineering. Greek city-states like Athens and Sparta competed for dominance in a series of conflicts known as the Peloponnesian War.

The Roman Empire grew from a small city on the Tiber River to control most of Europe, North Africa, and parts of the Middle East. Rome developed a sophisticated legal system that influenced laws across the Western world. Roman engineers built roads stretching over 80,000 kilometers, constructed aqueducts to carry fresh water across long distances, and developed architectural innovations including the arch, the vault, and the dome. Latin, the language of Rome, evolved into French, Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, and Romanian.

The Indus Valley Civilization flourished in what is now Pakistan and northwestern India from approximately 2500 to 1900 BCE. Cities like Mohenjo-daro and Harappa had advanced urban planning with grid street layouts and sophisticated covered drainage systems. Indus people traded with Mesopotamia and developed standardized weights and measures. Their script remains undeciphered.

Ancient China developed along the Yellow River and Yangtze River valleys. The Shang Dynasty, which ruled from approximately 1600 to 1046 BCE, produced the earliest Chinese writing on oracle bones used in divination rituals. The Zhou Dynasty followed and is associated with the philosophical teachings of Confucius, who emphasized virtue, family loyalty, and social harmony. Chinese civilization contributed silk production, papermaking, gunpowder, and the magnetic compass to world history.

The Aztec civilization dominated central Mexico from the 14th to 16th centuries. Their capital, Tenochtitlan, was built on an island in Lake Texcoco and held a population of around 200,000 people at its height. The Aztecs constructed floating gardens called chinampas to grow crops on lake surfaces. They maintained an extensive trading network and kept detailed calendar systems for tracking agriculture and religious events.

The Inca Empire, centered in the Andes Mountains of South America, was the largest pre-Columbian empire in the Americas. The Inca built a road network spanning over 40,000 kilometers across mountains and valleys, using relay runners to carry messages. They developed terrace farming on steep slopes to grow potatoes, maize, and quinoa at high altitudes. The city of Machu Picchu, built in the 15th century, sits at 2,430 meters above sea level and served as a royal estate and religious sanctuary.

Ancient Persia, under the Achaemenid Dynasty, created one of the largest empires in ancient history. Cyrus the Great founded the empire around 550 BCE and was known for his policy of tolerance toward the cultures and religions of conquered peoples. The Persians constructed the Royal Road, stretching 2,700 kilometers from Sardis to Susa, enabling rapid communication across the empire. Darius I introduced a standardized coinage system and divided the empire into provinces called satrapies, each administered by a governor.

The Phoenicians, based along the coast of modern Lebanon, were master seafarers and traders. They established colonies across the Mediterranean, including Carthage in North Africa, founded around 814 BCE. Their greatest contribution was the Phoenician alphabet, a 22-letter writing system that became the ancestor of Greek, Latin, Arabic, and Hebrew alphabets used today.
