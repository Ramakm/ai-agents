Climate science studies the long-term patterns of temperature, precipitation, wind, and other atmospheric conditions across Earth. The climate system involves five interconnected components: the atmosphere, the hydrosphere (water bodies), the cryosphere (ice and snow), the biosphere (living organisms), and the lithosphere (land surface). Changes in any one component affect all others, making climate a complex, dynamic system.

The greenhouse effect is the primary mechanism by which Earth maintains surface temperatures warm enough to support life. Certain gases in the atmosphere, called greenhouse gases, absorb outgoing infrared radiation from Earth's surface and re-radiate it back downward. The main greenhouse gases include water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone. Without any greenhouse effect, Earth's average surface temperature would be approximately minus 18 degrees Celsius rather than the current plus 15 degrees Celsius.

Since the industrial revolution began in the mid-1700s, human activities have significantly increased the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. Burning fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas releases carbon dioxide stored underground over millions of years. Deforestation removes trees that would otherwise absorb carbon dioxide through photosynthesis. Agricultural practices, including rice cultivation and livestock farming, release methane and nitrous oxide. The atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide has increased from approximately 280 parts per million before industrialization to over 420 parts per million today.

Global surface temperatures have increased by approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius since the pre-industrial period. This warming is not uniform across the globe. The Arctic region is warming at more than twice the global average rate, a phenomenon called Arctic amplification. This accelerated warming occurs because melting sea ice exposes darker ocean water, which absorbs more solar radiation than reflective ice, creating a positive feedback loop.

Rising global temperatures are causing measurable changes to Earth's ice. Arctic sea ice extent has declined by roughly 13 percent per decade since satellite observations began in 1979. The Greenland ice sheet and Antarctic ice sheet are losing mass at accelerating rates. Mountain glaciers around the world are retreating, threatening freshwater supplies for billions of people who depend on glacial meltwater during dry seasons.

Sea levels are rising as a result of two main processes: thermal expansion of warming ocean water and the addition of meltwater from glaciers and ice sheets. Global mean sea level has risen approximately 20 centimeters since 1900. The rate of rise has accelerated in recent decades. Current projections suggest sea levels could rise between 30 centimeters and one meter by 2100 depending on future greenhouse gas emissions, threatening coastal cities and low-lying island nations.

Extreme weather events are becoming more frequent and intense in a warming climate. Heatwaves are occurring more often and reaching higher peak temperatures. Heavy rainfall events are intensifying because warmer air holds more moisture. Tropical cyclones are expected to produce stronger peak winds and heavier precipitation. Droughts are becoming more severe in already dry regions as higher temperatures increase evaporation from soil and water surfaces.

The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015 by 196 countries, established a global framework to limit warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to limit warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius. Countries submitted nationally determined contributions outlining their emission reduction targets. Achieving these targets requires rapid transitions away from fossil fuels toward renewable energy sources such as solar, wind, and hydropower.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, known as the IPCC, was established in 1988 to assess scientific information about climate change. It produces comprehensive assessment reports summarizing research from thousands of scientists worldwide. The IPCC does not conduct original research but synthesizes published literature to inform policymakers about the state of climate science, impacts, and mitigation options.

Ocean acidification is a direct chemical consequence of rising atmospheric carbon dioxide. The oceans absorb roughly 25 percent of the carbon dioxide emitted by human activities each year. When carbon dioxide dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, lowering ocean pH. Since the industrial revolution, average ocean surface pH has decreased from 8.2 to 8.1, representing a 26 percent increase in acidity. This change threatens marine organisms that build shells and skeletons from calcium carbonate, including corals, oysters, clams, and some plankton species.
