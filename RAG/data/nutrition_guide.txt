Nutrition Guide: Eating for Health and Energy

Macronutrients
Macronutrients are the three major nutrients your body needs in large amounts to function properly. They are carbohydrates, proteins, and fats.

Carbohydrates are the body's primary source of energy. They are broken down into glucose, which fuels the brain and muscles. Complex carbohydrates found in whole grains, legumes, and vegetables are digested slowly and provide sustained energy. Simple carbohydrates found in sugar and processed foods cause rapid spikes in blood sugar. Adults should aim for 45-65% of total calories from carbohydrates.

Proteins are essential for building and repairing tissues, producing enzymes and hormones, and supporting immune function. Complete proteins containing all nine essential amino acids are found in animal products like meat, poultry, fish, eggs, and dairy. Plant-based sources like legumes, tofu, and quinoa can provide complete protein when combined correctly. The recommended daily intake is 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight for sedentary adults, and up to 2 grams per kilogram for athletes.

Fats are crucial for absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), protecting organs, and producing hormones. Unsaturated fats found in olive oil, avocados, nuts, and fatty fish are heart-healthy. Saturated fats found in red meat and dairy should be limited. Trans fats found in processed foods should be avoided entirely.

Micronutrients
Vitamins and minerals are micronutrients required in small amounts for vital body functions. Vitamin C found in citrus fruits and bell peppers supports immune function and collagen production. Vitamin D found in sunlight, fatty fish, and fortified foods is essential for bone health and immune function. Vitamin B12 found in animal products supports nerve function and red blood cell formation. Iron found in red meat, spinach, and lentils is required for oxygen transport in the blood. Calcium found in dairy, leafy greens, and tofu builds and maintains strong bones and teeth.

Hydration
Water is essential for every function in the human body. It regulates body temperature, transports nutrients, and removes waste products. Adults should drink approximately 2-3 litres of water per day, though needs vary based on activity level, climate, and body size. Dehydration even at 2% loss can impair cognitive performance and physical endurance. Signs of dehydration include dark urine, fatigue, headache, and decreased concentration.

Fibre
Dietary fibre is a type of carbohydrate that the body cannot fully digest. Soluble fibre found in oats, apples, and beans dissolves in water and helps lower cholesterol and blood sugar. Insoluble fibre found in whole grains and vegetables adds bulk to stool and promotes healthy digestion. Adults should aim for 25-38 grams of fibre per day. A high-fibre diet is associated with reduced risk of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and colorectal cancer.

Meal Planning
A balanced meal should include half a plate of vegetables and fruits, one quarter lean protein, and one quarter whole grains. Eating at regular intervals helps maintain stable blood sugar levels and prevents overeating. Preparing meals in advance reduces reliance on processed and fast foods. Reading nutrition labels helps you track calorie intake, added sugars, sodium, and trans fat content. Eating slowly and mindfully improves digestion and helps you recognise satiety signals earlier.

Superfoods
Certain foods are particularly dense in nutrients and health-promoting compounds. Blueberries are rich in antioxidants that protect cells from damage. Salmon provides omega-3 fatty acids that reduce inflammation and support brain health. Leafy greens like kale and spinach are packed with vitamins, minerals, and phytonutrients. Nuts and seeds provide healthy fats, protein, and fibre. Fermented foods like yogurt, kefir, and kimchi support gut microbiome health.
